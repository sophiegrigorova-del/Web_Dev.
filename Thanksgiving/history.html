Origins of Thanksgiving

Thanksgiving traces back to 1621, when the Pilgrims in Plymouth, Massachusetts celebrated a successful harvest. They shared a feast with the Wampanoag people, who helped them survive their first harsh winter.

President Abraham Lincoln declared Thanksgiving a national holiday in 1863 during the Civil War, to encourage unity and reflection among Americans.

Thanksgiving Traditions

Family Feasts: Roasted turkey, stuffing, cranberry sauce, mashed potatoes, and pumpkin pie are classic dishes shared with loved ones.
Parades: Cities hold parades; the Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade in New York City is the most famous, with floats, balloons, and performances.
Football: NFL games are a popular Thanksgiving pastime.
Giving Thanks: Expressing gratitude through personal reflection or shared stories at the table is central to the holiday.
Volunteering: Many people help feed the hungry or support charities, embodying the spirit of generosity.

Fun Facts About Thanksgiving

  • The first Thanksgiving feast lasted three days.
  • Turkey may not have been served at the first Thanksgiving.
  • Pumpkin pie became a common dessert in the 19th century.
  • The U.S. President pardons a turkey every year before Thanksgiving.
  • Many families have unique regional dishes like ham, seafood, or casseroles.
  • Thanksgiving is celebrated in Canada in October, and other countries have similar harvest festivals.

Reflection and Gratitude

Beyond food and traditions, Thanksgiving is about gratitude, family, and community. People use this time to reflect on what they are thankful for, reconnect with loved ones, and extend kindness to others.

Volunteering, sharing stories, and appreciating simple joys all continue the spirit of the first Thanksgiving and keep it relevant today.
